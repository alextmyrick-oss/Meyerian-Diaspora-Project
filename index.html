About this dataset
The Phipps Staff Conference Record
Description

The Phipps Staff Conference archive documents 2,515 clinical sessions held at the Henry Phipps Psychiatric Clinic between 1931 and 1944. Each entry records the case folder reference, session date, session contents with diagnosis where available, and the presenting physician. The record is organized by presenter folder and spans the full active life of the clinic under Adolf Meyer's directorship and the early years of his successors.

Research significance

The Staff Conference record is one of the most detailed surviving documents of Meyer's psychobiology in institutional practice. It captures not only what was presented but who presented it, revealing the social structure of knowledge transmission at the clinic. The fourteen-year span covers Meyer's final decade of active leadership, his retirement in 1941, and the wartime disruption that effectively ended regular conference activity by 1943.

For the Meyerian Diaspora project, the conference record provides direct evidence of how Meyer's methods were transmitted to trainees who would carry them to institutions across North America — making the Phipps Clinic not merely a treatment centre but a training engine for a generation of psychiatrists.

Interpretive significance

The data reveals a clinic operating on a strict academic rhythm: sessions ran from September through May with almost complete cessation in June, July, and August. This confirms the Phipps Clinic's identity as an academic institution rather than a purely clinical one — the conference calendar followed the university term, not hospital operational needs.

Meyer's personal participation peaked in 1938 (29 sessions) before dropping sharply in 1940–1941, two years before his official retirement. This suggests a gradual, deliberate withdrawal from active teaching rather than a sudden institutional change — a pattern consistent with the transitional nature of his final years at Phipps.

Sessions by semester: Across all fourteen years, fall sessions (September–December) consistently outnumbered spring sessions (January–June) in the early part of the record, but the two halves become more balanced from the mid-1930s onward. The fall semester typically marked the arrival of new trainees and the introduction of fresh case material, while spring sessions tended toward review and follow-up. The sharp compression of both semesters after 1941 reflects the combined effect of Meyer's retirement and wartime staffing losses.

Sessions by month (all years combined)

Sessions by month: Aggregated across all fourteen years, the monthly distribution makes the academic calendar structure unmistakable. September, October, March, January, and February are the busiest months, each accounting for over 250 sessions. June drops to just 37 and July to 12 — the summer recess was nearly total. August records a single session across the entire fourteen-year span. The pattern confirms that the Staff Conferences were an academic institution's teaching exercise, not a routine hospital activity running year-round.

Interpretive significance

The most striking finding is Adolf Meyer's own position in the presenter record. Despite being the clinic's director and the dominant figure in American psychiatry, Meyer presented only 12 clinical cases across fourteen years — his 188 sessions were overwhelmingly teaching conversations, reports, and supervisory commentary. This confirms that Meyer's role at Phipps was pedagogical rather than clinical.

The temporal segmentation reveals a clinic in generational transition. Figures dominant in the early period — Barbour, Watters, Miller — are almost entirely absent from the middle and late years, replaced by a cohort whose careers extended well beyond Phipps: Lemkau, Lidz, Rennie, the Leightons. The session type breakdown further reveals that certain figures — Richter, Diethelm, Gantt — were research or administrative contributors rather than clinical case presenters, a distinction invisible in simple session counts alone.

Guest presenters

65 individual visitors · 95 sessions total · 1931–1944

Beyond the regular Phipps staff, the conference record documents 95 sessions given by visiting scholars, clinicians, and researchers from outside the clinic. These guest presentations reveal the intellectual range of Meyer's network — from leading European psychoanalysts to anthropologists, neurologists, and industrial researchers. The presence of figures such as Karen Horney, Frieda Fromm-Reichmann, Jean Piaget, Margaret Mead, and Pierre Janet at Phipps Staff Conferences is itself a significant finding, demonstrating that Meyer's clinic operated as a hub for broad cross-disciplinary exchange rather than a closed psychiatric training programme.

What this shows

The diagnosis visualizations present the clinical content of case presentations and reviews across two analytical lenses. The first uses modern diagnostic categories to map the Phipps caseload onto recognizable psychiatric terminology. The second preserves and analyses Meyer's own psychobiological vocabulary — the ergasic reaction types that he developed as an alternative to categorical diagnosis — which accounts for a significant portion of the record and is presented here on its own terms.

Interpretive significance

Depression and mood disorders dominate the Phipps clinical record across all fourteen years, accounting for more than 37% of identifiable case presentations. The relative decline of schizophrenia/dementia praecox presentations after 1934 — from 48 cases that year to 15 by 1938 — may reflect a genuine shift in the clinic's case mix or a change in diagnostic labelling practices as Meyerian terminology became more prevalent among trainee presenters.

The Meyerian terminology chart reveals something that modern categorical mapping obscures entirely: the conference record contains hundreds of presentations using Meyer's ergasic reaction vocabulary — thymergasic (mood-related), parergasic (schizophrenia-spectrum), merergasic (neurotic), dysergasic (organic), anergasic (withdrawal/catatonic). This vocabulary was not imprecision — it was a deliberate theoretical framework resisting the categorical psychiatry that would eventually replace it. The persistence of this language throughout the 1930s and into the 1940s is direct evidence of Meyer's intellectual influence on his trainees' diagnostic practice.

What this shows

The session type visualizations break down all 2,515 conference entries by the format of each session, extracted from the Contents field. The overall donut chart shows the distribution across all years. The three period charts reveal how the balance of session formats shifted across the clinic's early (1931–1935), middle (1936–1939), and late (1940–1944) periods.

Interpretive significance

The most revealing shift across the three periods is not in case presentation itself — which remains dominant throughout — but in the formats that surround it. Teaching/Conversation, Meyer's signature session type, accounts for 10% of all sessions in the early period and drops steadily to 4% by the final years. These were not ordinary discussions: Meyer's "conversations" were extended theoretical monologues, often running across several consecutive sessions, in which he worked through his psychobiological framework in real time with his trainees. Their decline tracks almost precisely with his physical withdrawal from daily clinic life after 1938. The compensating rise of Case Discussion to 20% in 1940–1944 marks a structural inversion: where Meyer's era placed one authoritative voice at the centre of every case, Whitehorn's era distributed interpretive authority across the assembled group. The conference format itself became more collegial as the clinic's intellectual centre of gravity shifted.

Equally telling is the collapse of Review/Update of Case — from 12% in both the early and middle periods to just 4% in 1940–1944. In Meyer's system, follow-up was not optional: trainees were expected to return to previously presented patients, tracking treatment outcomes, revising diagnoses, and accounting for changes in clinical status over weeks and months. The longitudinal follow-up built into the case record was a direct expression of his psychobiological commitment to the patient as a developing biography rather than a static diagnosis. Its near-disappearance in the final period suggests that this dimension of Meyer's clinical pedagogy did not survive the transition to Whitehorn's leadership — or that wartime conditions made sustained follow-up difficult to sustain in practice.

Overall distribution: Case Presentation (1,449 sessions, 58%) was the conference's foundational format: a trainee presented a patient and Meyer offered commentary and diagnosis. Review/Update of Case (236, 9%) tracked those patients longitudinally across weeks and months. Together, these two case-based formats account for two-thirds of all sessions. The remaining third captures the conference's broader intellectual life — Teaching/Conversation (187, 7%), Talk/Lecture (150, 6%), Report/Summary (137, 5%), and Case Discussion (146, 6%) — with Special/Anniversary, Commentary, and Film/Demonstration sessions concentrated almost entirely in the 1940–1944 period.

By time period: In the early period (1931–1935), case presentations dominated at 67% and Teaching/Conversation occupied a substantial 10%, reflecting Meyer's active pedagogical style and his habit of extending case discussions into wide-ranging theoretical monologues. The middle period (1936–1939) holds a similar profile, though Report/Summary rises as trainees began presenting original research alongside clinical cases. The late period (1940–1944) is the most structurally distinct: Case Presentation drops to 51%, Case Discussion rises sharply to 20% under Whitehorn's more collegial leadership, and Special/Anniversary sessions cluster around the clinic's institutional commemorations — while Review/Update of Case collapses from 12% to 4%, marking the end of Meyer's longitudinal follow-up culture.

Session types by year

Navigate year by year to see how session formats shifted across the full 1931–1944 record

Year by year: Where the three-period donuts show broad structural shifts, the year-by-year view makes the granular texture of those changes visible. Teaching/Conversation reaches its peak in 1931 at 16% — Meyer's first full year running the conferences — and never again approaches that proportion, declining gradually through the 1930s and collapsing to a single session in 1941, Whitehorn's first year as director. Review/Update of Case, the format that embodied Meyer's commitment to longitudinal follow-up, is notably volatile across the middle years rather than declining smoothly, spiking in 1933–1934 before settling back — suggesting that longitudinal follow-up practice was unevenly applied depending on the cohort and caseload of a given year. The 1938 profile is the most anomalous in the record: an unusually large Talk/Lecture and Other segment, reflecting both the clinic's 25th Anniversary preparations and the May 1938 bulk-citation session. From 1942 onward the data should be read with caution — the dramatic rise of Case Discussion in that year reflects Whitehorn's restructured conference format but also the incomplete archival record for the final period, which does not fully document the sessions that continued through 1944.

By presenter: Each presenter's profile reflects their role in the clinic hierarchy. First-year trainees show profiles dominated almost entirely by Case Presentation; Teaching/Conversation entries typically mark sessions where their case sparked a broader discussion led by Meyer. What this shows

Three complementary analyses of the Staff Conference record. The diagnostic revision flow traces cases where the recorded diagnosis shifted between a patient's first and final conference session, revealing the conferences as a space of active diagnostic negotiation. The session rhythm charts show how conference activity was distributed across the week and across the academic year. Meyer's teaching topics analyses the 102 Teaching/Conversation sessions he initiated, identifying the recurring themes he returned to when directing clinical discussions beyond the immediate case.

Interpretive significance

Taken together these three analyses document the texture of clinical life at Phipps. The 69 cross-category diagnostic revisions — particularly the bidirectional flow between Schizophrenia and Depression — show that diagnosis at Phipps was not a fixed label applied at presentation but a working hypothesis revised as the clinical picture evolved across multiple sessions. The session rhythm confirms the intensity of the conference culture: a six-day working week, with September the single most active month as each new cohort commenced. And the dominance of student training in Meyer's own teaching sessions — nearly half of all his Teaching/Conversations explicitly concerned how psychiatry should be taught — reveals his primary preoccupation in these years was less with treating individual patients than with producing a generation of psychiatrists who thought in his image.

Diagnostic revision flows, 1931–1944

227 cross-category revisions across all multi-session case folders · curve width ∝ case count · colour = starting diagnosis · hover any curve for count

Diagnostic revision flow: The dominant flow is Schizophrenia to Depression (54 cases), followed by Depression to Schizophrenia (37) — a bidirectional current that accounts for nearly two-fifths of all revisions and reflects a clinical boundary Meyer consistently refused to resolve by categorical fiat. Anxiety to Depression (24) and Depression to Anxiety (18) form the second-largest pair. Organic conditions flow disproportionately toward Depression (12 cases) and Schizophrenia (8), consistent with the difficulty of distinguishing functional from organic presentations before modern neuroimaging. The Psychopathic, Child, and Dissociative categories are more stable as revision destinations, appearing mainly as endpoints for cases originally recorded under affective or psychotic labels.

Session rhythm: Monday's lower session count (275 vs. ~470 for Tuesday–Friday) likely reflects Monday morning rounds or administrative time reserved outside the conference schedule. Saturday's 390 sessions confirm the Phipps conferences ran six days a week — a significant clinical and pedagogical commitment. The monthly pattern makes the academic year cycle unmistakable: September is the most active single month (310 sessions) as each new cohort commenced, June collapses to 37 as the year closed, and August is effectively absent (1 session across all fourteen years). The Christmas period is visible as a relative dip in December, and the slight May spike corresponds to the end-of-year review sessions when trainees updated their longitudinal cases before departing.

Meyer's teaching conversations — recurring themes

102 Teaching/Conversation sessions initiated by Meyer · classified by primary topic from the Contents field

Meyer's teaching topics: Nearly half of Meyer's Teaching/Conversation sessions (46 of 102) explicitly concerned the training of psychiatrists — student orientation, examination of second-year students, course outlines, and the formal structure of psychiatric education. Psychobiology and personality theory account for a further eight, suggesting his theoretical framework was invoked less frequently in conversation than might be expected from its prominence in his published writing. The near-absence of brain anatomy (zero sessions) and psychoanalysis (one session) is equally striking: by the 1930s Meyer had moved decisively away from neuroanatomical reductionism and maintained a studied distance from Freudian theory. What dominated was pedagogy — how to teach psychiatrists to think, more than what to think about.

About this project

The Meyerian Diaspora Research Project

This interactive site presents data and visualizations developed in support of a doctoral dissertation examining the proliferation of psychiatric reform ideas originating with Johns Hopkins psychiatrist Adolf Meyer, and their conveyance through a network of trained physicians to institutions across North America. The dissertation argues that Meyer's influence operated through a diaspora of psychiatrists who carried his clinical methods and institutional model outward from Baltimore, reshaping the structure of the discipline as they went. Canada serves as the dissertation's primary case study — a precise cross-section through which to examine where Meyerian influence was strong, where it was limited, and how it was adapted as it moved across institutional and national contexts.

The Henry Phipps Psychiatric Clinic

The Henry Phipps Psychiatric Clinic, opened at Johns Hopkins Hospital in 1913, was the institutional centrepiece of Meyer's career. Designed as a model facility for the new scientific psychiatry and funded by philanthropist Henry Phipps Jr., the clinic functioned simultaneously as a treatment centre, a research laboratory, and a training programme. The psychiatrists Meyer trained at Phipps became the primary vehicle through which his reform vision spread — carrying his psychobiological framework into hospitals, medical schools, and clinics across the continent. The visualizations on this site draw on the clinic's Staff Conference archive, which documents fourteen years of the training programme Meyer built and the institutional culture it produced.

Data and Methodology — Staff Conferences

The Staff Conference dataset comprises 2,515 session records drawn from the Adolf Meyer Collection at the Alan Mason Chesney Medical Archives, Johns Hopkins University. The records are organized by presenter folder and span the full active period of the conferences from 1931 to 1944. Presenter names have been fully resolved from the original "Dr. Lastname" format in the finding aid using two reference sources: a 320-person cohort name list and the Phipps Staff Roster, a spreadsheet tracking individual JHUMS positions by year. Where two persons sharing a last name both held positions at Phipps during a given year and no further distinguishing information was available, both names are noted and the attribution flagged as unresolvable.

Diagnoses were extracted from the Contents field and classified under both a set of modern diagnostic categories and Meyer's original ergasic reaction vocabulary. The Meyerian terminology analysis preserves the original diagnostic language of the record rather than displacing it with retrospective modern equivalents — both systems are maintained in parallel, reflecting the historical specificity of Meyer's clinical vocabulary.

Session types were classified from the Contents field using a controlled vocabulary applied consistently across all 2,515 entries. Presenter role classifications — Director, Instructor rank and above, Resident and Assistant in Psychiatry — are drawn directly from the JHUMS positions recorded in the Phipps Staff Roster for each year of the conference period.

Additional datasets are in preparation and will be incorporated into this site as research progresses, including a prosopography of Meyer's trained cohort and career trajectory data for Phipps Clinic staff. Each new dataset will be documented in a separate section here.
